# RTFM

A practical manual for interacting with me

This writing is a guide into the conditions under whcih I tend to think clearly, communicate well, and to do good work.
Therefore it also acts like a map to show the conditions and situations under which I am likely to become harder to work with.
This document is not a demand that everyone should adapt to me. Read it like a 1 + 1 = 3.

## TL;DR version

I work best when the goal is clear, the work is interesting, and the next action is concrete.

I can perform very well when I understand the problem, care about the outcome, and have room to think, build, reason, or solve. I struggle more with vague obligations, long-running routine tasks, loose follow-up, hidden expectations, and work that depends on remembering many small details at the right time.

Even if it is not politically correct, I will always share my professional opinion.

If you want the best out of me:

- Be direct
- Be specific
- Put important things in writing

## Communication

### What Works Well

Use direct language. I prefer clarity over cushioning. If something is wrong, blocked, risky, late, unclear, or frustrating, say that plainly.

Make the actual ask explicit. A message like "Can you look at this?" can mean review, approve, debug, estimate, rewrite, decide, or just be aware. I do better with "Please review this for correctness by Friday" or "I need a yes/no decision."

Separate facts, opinions, and requests. I respond well when I can tell which part is evidence, which part is interpretation, and which part needs action.

Give me the reason. I am much more engaged when I understand the why behind a decision, constraint, or request. "Because the customer needs it" is useful. "Because process" usually is not.

## What Does Not Work Well

Hints, implication, and indirect dissatisfaction are unreliable. If you expect me to infer the real issue from tone or subtext, there is a decent chance I will miss it or focus on the wrong part.

Long verbal instructions are fragile. I may understand them in the moment and still lose details later. If it matters, write it down.

Ambiguous ownership creates drift. If everyone kind of owns something, assume I may not treat it as mine unless that has been made explicit.

### Meetings

Meetings are best when they have a purpose, an owner, and a decision path.

I am useful in meetings where we are solving a problem, making a decision, reviewing something concrete, or surfacing risks. I am much less useful in meetings that exist mainly to keep everyone loosely synchronized without clear outcomes.

Good meeting patterns:

- Send context beforehand if the topic is complex.
- Start with the decision or outcome needed.
- Name who owns each follow-up.
- Put action items in writing before the meeting ends.
- If my input is needed, say which kind: technical opinion, decision, risk review, estimate, or brainstorming.

If I have to work with bad tooling (ie. Jira, Windows), my productivity hurts.

## Work style

Coming from a QA background, I am automatically drawn to fixing issues on the People, Processes, Technology axes.
This means that I will do what it takes to make things happen, where others give in and accept.
I don't "just accept", which can give some tension. Don't take it personal, because I do not mean it personal.
It's the fire / passion that you meet. Together we will make things better.

Useful ways to work with me:

- Give me a problem to own, not just a list of chores.
- Define success clearly.
- Keep constraints visible.
- Use written tickets, checklists, docs, or shared notes.
- Use deadlines when they are real, not as vague pressure.

Useful ways to work with me:

- Use written tickets, checklists, docs, or shared notes.
- When questions are short or when needed a conversation: use Slack (or Teams).
- Use deadlines when they are real, not as vague pressure.

If something is important but boring, the structure matters more. A clear checklist, a short deadline, or an external review point can make the difference between "done" and "somehow still not done."

## Planning And Follow-Through

I use tools because memory is not a reliable system. Calendars, reminders, written notes, tickets, and visible task lists help turn intention into action.

Please do not treat "I told you once" as a robust delivery mechanism for important information.

Good patterns:

- Put dates and times in calendar invites.
- Put decisions and commitments in writing.
- Use one source of truth when possible.
- Make the next action visible.
- Follow up with a concise reminder if something matters.

Bad patterns:

- Relying on a hallway conversation.
- Assuming I will remember an implied deadline.
- Spreading obligations across chat, email, voice, and memory.
- Treating a vague "sometime soon" as a plan.

## Feedback

I prefer feedback that is direct, specific, and actionable.

Good feedback:

- "This section is too abstract. Please add a concrete example."
- "You interrupted twice in that meeting. Let the other person finish before responding."
- "The solution works, but the maintenance cost is too high because of X."

Less useful feedback:

- "Be more mindful."
- "This feels off."
- "You should know what I mean."

If you think I am being too forceful, too blunt, too detailed, or too persistent, say so directly. I may not always notice the social effect quickly enough in the moment, especially when I am focused on the content. My mouth is faster than my brain, and sometimes I'll speak out of turn because else I will forget what I wanted to say.

### Disagreement and Conflict

I can become attached to a line of reasoning when I think the logic matters. This can look like stubbornness, intensity, or unwillingness to move on.

What usually helps:

- Bring the disagreement back to the goal.
- Name the tradeoff we are deciding between.
- Ask what evidence would change my mind.
- Timebox the discussion if we are stuck.
- Separate "you are wrong" from "we are choosing a different constraint."

I respect strong arguments. I do not need people to agree with me. I do need the reasoning to be explicit enough that I can understand what is being optimized.

## Sensory Load And Environment

Noise and repeated small sensory irritations can consume more attention than I want them to. Some sounds or environments may make me impatient, distracted, or eager to leave.

This is not always visible from the outside. If I move seats, use headphones, step away, or become quieter, it may be an attempt to manage input rather than a reaction to the people around me.

Helpful defaults:

- I'll use headphones when focus matters.
- Prefer quieter spaces for complex conversations.
- Do not take it personally if I need to change location.
- For important topics, avoid trying to discuss them in noisy or chaotic settings. In noisy environments with competing sounds I literally cannot hear what you're saying.

## Social Interaction

I can talk too long about things I find interesting. I can also be too quick to jump in, correct, solve, or add context.

If this happens, it is fine to interrupt the pattern directly:

- "Short version?"
- "Can you pause there?"
- "I need a decision, not the full model."
- "Let's come back to that later."
- "I want to finish my thought first."

I would rather be redirected clearly than have someone silently disengage.

## How To Ask Me For Something

A good request contains:

- The outcome you want.
- Why it matters.
- When it is needed.
- What constraints apply.
- Whether you want speed, quality, exploration, or a final decision.

Example:

> Please review this proposal for technical risk by Thursday afternoon. I do not need wording edits; I need to know whether the approach will hold up in production and what the biggest unknowns are.

That kind of request is much easier for me to act on than:

> Can you take a look?

## What To Do If I Am Stuck

If I am avoiding, delaying, circling, or overthinking something, these interventions often help:

- Ask me to define the next physical action.
- Reduce the scope.
- Ask for a rough first pass.
- Create a short deadline.
- Pair for 20 minutes.
- Ask what information is missing.
- Ask what part feels pointless, risky, or unclear.

## What I Am Responsible For

I am responsible for how I show up, how I affect people, and how I manage my own systems.

You can expect me to:

- Own my commitments.
- Ask for clarification when I notice ambiguity.
- Repair when I have been too blunt, too intense, or too absent.
- Keep improving the systems that help me function well.

## Best Defaults

If in doubt:

- Write it down.
- Be direct.
- Make the next action clear.
- Explain the why.
- Use concrete deadlines.
- Give feedback early.
- Assume intensity is often focus, not aggression.
- Ask for the short version when you need it.

The goal is simple: fewer hidden expectations, fewer dropped details, better work, and less friction.
